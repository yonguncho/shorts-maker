# Shorts Retention & Attention Principles

Category: retention
Source: internal-knowledge-seed
Scope: US-stock YouTube Shorts, mascot + data style, no real people.

## The retention curve shape
A Short's retention curve is read in four zones. 0–1s decides if they stop scrolling (the thumbnail-in-motion). 1–3s decides if they commit (curiosity gap). 3s–midpoint is the steady bleed where weak scenes cause swipes. Midpoint–end is where a pattern interrupt and an open-loop payoff recover viewers. Design each zone deliberately; do not treat the video as a flat sequence.

## First-frame rule (0–1s)
The opening frame — the literal first second, the 0–1s window — must contain at least one of: motion, a large number, or the mascot reacting. In that first second, a static, text-only opening frame is read as "nothing happening" and loses the viewer before they parse a word. The first frame should show the mascot mid-reaction with a big number already on screen and something moving (count-up or Ken Burns), so the first second wins the scroll-stop instead of bleeding the view.

## Curiosity gap (0–3s)
Reveal part of the conclusion immediately, then withhold the reason. "ARM ripped +15.73% — and earnings had nothing to do with it." The viewer now has an open loop they must close, which carries them past the 3s commit point. Never open with backstory or definitions; open with the payoff fragment.

## Average percentage viewed (APV) is the master metric
APV (average view duration divided by length) above ~75% is the practical threshold for the algorithm to push a Short to more feeds. CTR gets the first impression; APV decides whether it scales. Every scene must justify its seconds against APV: cut filler, keep one message per scene, keep motion constant.

## Static-frame death trigger
Any on-screen moment held perfectly still for more than ~0.5s reads as "the video is over" and triggers a swipe. There must always be motion: Ken Burns on every background, sine idle on the mascot, count-ups on numbers, draw-on for charts. Even during a held caption, the background should drift.

## Audio change outperforms visual change
A beat drop, whoosh, ding, or alarm re-captures a drifting viewer more strongly than a silent visual cut. Snap scene starts and key reveals to audio events. A montage with no audio punctuation feels flat and bleeds viewers even if the visuals change.

## One message per scene, 3–4s minimum
Humans need 3–4 seconds to absorb one discrete fact. Splitting a fact across sub-second cuts looks energetic but the information does not stick. Use sub-cuts for motion energy, but keep one core message per scene held long enough to read (~5s for data scenes).

## Progress signal keeps viewers to the end
A progress gauge or dot row ("almost done") exploits the completion bias: viewers stay to finish a thing they can see ending. Use a 12-dot indicator matching the 12 scenes; fill it as the video advances.

## Pattern interrupt at the midpoint
Mid-video is where casual viewers drop. A sudden claim or visual that contradicts expectation resets attention: "ARM is up 15%. Don't chase it." or a hard cut to a red siren. Place one deliberate interrupt around 50–60% of the runtime.

## Open loop and payoff
Promise something at the start that is only resolved at the end: "Stay for the one chip stock that hasn't moved yet." The unresolved loop is the single strongest tool for full-video retention. The payoff line must literally answer the opening hook in the last 3 seconds.

## Reading speed and on-screen text budget
Viewers read ~15 characters/second comfortably on a phone. A two-line caption of 18 chars/line (36 chars) needs ~2.4s minimum on screen. Do not flash dense text faster than it can be read; either shorten it or hold it longer.

## Scroll-stop triggers (thumbnail-in-motion)
Even inside the feed, the first half-second acts as a live thumbnail. Strong stoppers: a huge percentage, a bold question, red/green contrast, a face stand-in (mascot). Weak stoppers: a slow logo fade, a calm chart, paragraphs of text.

## Re-watch and looping
Shorts loop. A clean visual match between the last frame and the first frame encourages an accidental re-watch, inflating APV above 100% on some views. End on a frame that flows back into the hook (e.g., the mascot gesture that opened it).

## Comment-bait for session time
A mild, honest question in the closing ("Which lagging name are you watching?") drives comments, which raise session time and signal engagement. Avoid manipulative or advice-seeking prompts; keep it observational.

## Hook–retention alignment
The hook must promise exactly what the body delivers. A clickbait hook that the body fails to pay off spikes CTR but craters APV and trains the algorithm against the channel. Truthful, specific hooks compound; exaggerated ones decay.

## Density vs length tradeoff
Shorts cap at 60s (90s pushes into regular-video treatment). Twelve scenes at ~5s each fills 60s with one message per scene. Going shorter per scene to add scenes destroys comprehension; going longer drops below the scene count needed for perceived value. 12 scenes / ~55–60s / one message each is the equilibrium.
