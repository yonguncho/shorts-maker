# Trust-Signal Visualization Devices

Category: trust
Source: internal-knowledge-seed
Thesis: credibility is shown, not claimed. The channel's edge is "this one actually reads the data." Put proof on every frame.

## Persistent data chips
Always show small pill chips: [RSI {value}] [Vol {x}× avg] [52w {position}]. Their constant presence — even when the scene is about something else — signals continuous data literacy. Update values per ticker; never fake them.

## Source watermark, always on
A persistent footer cites sources: "Data: Yahoo Finance · Source: {news_source}". Citation is the number-one trust signal and simultaneously a copyright defense (you are reporting, with attribution, not republishing).

## Timestamp the data
The header shows "As of {date}, {time} ET". Stocks are time-sensitive; an explicit timestamp blocks "stale re-upload" suspicion and frames the content as a dated briefing rather than evergreen hype.

## State the comparison baseline
Never show "+15%" naked. Show "+15% vs SPX +0.3%" — relative strength is the number a real trader evaluates. Comparison to the index (or to the sector) demonstrates you understand context, not just the raw move.

## Show the methodology
Tiny captions that expose the method build trust: "Period: 3M · close", "Volume vs 30-day average", "RSI(14)". Revealing how a number was computed signals a real process and pre-empts "where did this come from."

## Mandatory risk scene
One scene always states a single honest risk: overheated chart, unconfirmed guidance, single-customer concentration, thin volume. Balance instantly separates the channel from the 99% that are purely bullish, drives thoughtful comments, and defends against advice-liability. Omitting it should fail the build.

## Confidence labeling (durability badge)
Show DURABLE (bull) vs TEMPORARY (alert) with a one-line basis. Hedged confidence ("likely durable", "probably temporary") reads as more expert than false certainty. The badge turns a vague vibe into a stated, falsifiable judgment.

## Classification labels
Tag the catalyst: "Based on: earnings / guidance / analyst / sympathy". Naming the classification basis shows a framework rather than a guess and lets the viewer audit the reasoning.

## Volume verdict, explicit
Do not merely display volume — render a verdict. ≥1.5× the 30-day average = "Conviction move"; <1× = "Suspect — low volume". Stating the judgment (not just the bar) is the differentiator that makes the channel feel like a desk, not a feed.

## Cite the source in voice, once
The narration references the source aloud at least once ("According to Yahoo Finance...", "Per the 247wallst report..."). An audio citation reinforces the on-screen watermark and reaches viewers who are listening, not reading.

## Disclaimer, persistent and spoken
"Not financial advice. Educational purposes." stays on screen the whole video (small footer) and is spoken once in the close. This is both an ethics/credibility signal and legal hygiene for US audiences.

## Balance in language
Pair any bullish observation with the counter-condition: "momentum is real, but it needs volume to hold." This even-handed framing is itself a trust signal; one-sided cheerleading reads as a pump.

## Specificity over adjectives
Replace "huge volume" with "1.66× the 30-day average"; replace "soaring" with "+15.73%". Concrete, sourced numbers do the persuading. Adjectives without numbers are the signature of low-trust content.

## Eight-signal stack
Target roughly eight visible trust signals per video: data chips, source watermark, timestamp, comparison baseline, methodology caption, risk scene, durability badge, spoken citation. Stacked, they create an overwhelming "this is real" impression that no amount of polish alone can fake — and that beats face-driven channels on credibility.
